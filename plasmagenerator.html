Inertial Electrostatic Confinement Plasma Generator

This device was built out of mainly scrap parts over 6 months (I am still upgrading it daily). It creates plasma through a process called inertial electrostatic confinement, or IEC. The basic layout consists of a vacuum chamber with an anode and cathode, a vacuum system to generate the vacuum, a HV power supply, and a gas injection system. The voltage potential between the central cathode wire cage and the chamber anode (-2.1kV to -8kV depending on which power supply I use), ionizes the gas and accelerates it inward, giving some of the individual ions temperatures in the 10s of millions of degrees. Some of these ions collide in the center, forming a plasma. Currently I have created Nitrogen plasma with the trace amounts of remaining air after a vacuum pump down, and regular hydrogen, synthesized by combining Aluminum shavings and 0.1 M Sodium Hydroxide. See images of ambient air nitrogen plasma and general setup below. The plasma in the pictures was created at ~2.1kV with a chamber pressure of ~50 microns. You can see a video of the device here.

Vacuum System

The vacuum system for the plasma generator is a Welch 8920A oil sealed rotary vane pump. I fully disassembled and refurbished it over the course of 6 months after getting it off ebay in terrible condition ( It was bad because I bought it for 200$ and they are usually sold for over $3000). This pump was connected to the chamber with a few feet 3/4in ID PVC tubing using metal compression sleeves. (The gas ballast stays closed during operation). The chamber pressure is measured with a VG64 digital vacuum gauge that is connected to the chamber by a series of flange adapters sealed with epoxy. The guage's 0.5 micron resolution gives me a really good picture of the true pressure conditions. See internal mechanics, homemade part replacements, and before and after pics below

Cage and HV vacuum feedthrough

I made my own HV feedthrough that feeds the cathode into the chamber without shorting it to the chamber or causing leaks. I did this by drilling a 1Â½" hole in an old grinding wheel from a scrap dump, placing the stainless steel cage I built inside of it, sealing the bottom, and pouring in marine epoxy to fill the hole. The epoxy binds to the edges of everything it touches forming a seal, and its dielectric properties prevent a short. In a more recent generation I used Sodium Silicate instead of epoxy because it's a better dielectric, completely heat resistant (the cage gets really hot from the plasma), and off-gasses less, helping me reach a higher vacuum. The most up to date iteration is liquid-cooled, allowing for my demo fusor to operate for hours at a time

Chamber (Generation 2)

The front window is 1" by 7" by 7" acrylic from a plastics store. The custom bolts I made compress the flange-feedthrough and the window onto the gaskets on both sides of the chamber, forming a seal. The gaskets are made from EDPM synthetic rubber. I cut them out of a large sheet with scissors. The chamber is made of 5 inch ID stainless steel tubing with 3 outlets of 3/4in OD stainless tubing welded on. Credit to Calvin Holmes for the welding. The small hairline fractures in the welds were sealed with Sodium Silicate. The third outlet is sealed off in the plasma images but is used for gas injection.

Chamber (Generation 1)

The first generation chamber was different in that it had a far more archaic method for flange-gasket compression. I connected 4 aluminum bars to 4 sets of standoffs, forming crude brackets that I slid on the chamber and cinched down. There were a number of issues with this setup that led me to make the improvements seen in generation 2 above. The first was ease of use. The brackets were extremely tedious to get on and off, meaning it was very hard to switch between the multiple feedthrough flanges I had made. The second, more important issue, was uniform gasket compression and leak detection. The brackets were hard to keep in one place on the faces of the chamber, meaning that it was almost impossible to compress the gasket perfectly. This made leak detection a major issue because I never knew whether the cause of the leak was bracket alignment or an actual connection. This was resolved in the 2nd generation by replacing the old feedthrough flanges with larger square aluminum plates that could accept 4 fixed bolts.

2.1kv DC @200 mA power supply

This 1st generation HV system consists of a variable water resistor for current control, a variac, and a center tapped 2.1kv transformer I sourced from a microwave. This feeds into a full bridge rectifier made from diodes I also took out of a microwave.
