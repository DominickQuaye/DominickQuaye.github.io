Inertial Electrostatic Confinement Plasma Generator

This device was built out of mainly scrap parts over 6 months (I am still upgrading it daily). It creates and confines plasma through a process called inertial electrostatic confinement, or IEC.(Note I will refer to this device as a plasma generator, Fusor, or Demo Fusor) The basic layout consists of a vacuum chamber with an anode and cathode, a vacuum system to generate the vacuum, a HV power supply, and a gas injection system. The voltage potential between the central cathode wire cage and the chamber anode (-2.1kV to -8kV depending on which power supply I use), ionizes the gas and accelerates it inward, giving some of the individual ions temperatures in the 10s of millions of degrees. Some of these ions collide in the center, forming a plasma. Currently I have created Nitrogen plasma with the trace amounts of remaining air after a vacuum pump down, and regular hydrogen, synthesized by combining Aluminum shavings and 0.1 M Sodium Hydroxide. See images of ambient air nitrogen plasma and general setup below. The plasma in the pictures was created at ~2.1kV with a chamber pressure of ~50 microns. You can see a video of the device here.

Vacuum System

The vacuum system for the plasma generator is a Welch 8920A oil sealed rotary vane pump. I fully disassembled and refurbished it over the course of 6 months after getting it off ebay in terrible condition ( It was bad because I bought it for 200$ and they are usually sold for over $3000). This pump was connected to the chamber with a few feet 3/4in ID PVC tubing using metal compression sleeves. (The gas ballast stays closed during operation). The chamber pressure is measured with a VG64 digital vacuum gauge that is connected to the chamber by a series of flange adapters sealed with epoxy. The guage's 0.5 micron resolution gives me a really good picture of the true pressure conditions. See internal mechanics, homemade part replacements, and before and after pics below

Cage and HV vacuum feedthrough

I made my own HV feedthrough that feeds the cathode into the chamber without shorting it to the chamber or causing leaks. I did this by drilling a 1Â½" hole in an old grinding wheel from a scrap dump, placing the stainless steel cage I built inside of it, sealing the bottom, and pouring in marine epoxy to fill the hole. The epoxy binds to the edges of everything it touches forming a seal, and its dielectric properties prevent a short. In a more recent generation I used Sodium Silicate instead of epoxy because it's a better dielectric, completely heat resistant (the cage gets really hot from the plasma), and off-gasses less, helping me reach a higher vacuum. The most up to date iteration is liquid-cooled, allowing for my demo fusor to operate for hours at a time

Chamber (Generation 2)

The front window is 1" by 7" by 7" acrylic from a plastics store. The custom bolts I made compress the flange-feedthrough and the window onto the gaskets on both sides of the chamber, forming a seal. The gaskets are made from EDPM synthetic rubber. I cut them out of a large sheet with scissors. The chamber is made of 5 inch ID stainless steel tubing with 3 outlets of 3/4in OD stainless tubing welded on. Credit to Calvin Holmes for the welding. The small hairline fractures in the welds were sealed with Sodium Silicate. The third outlet is sealed off in the plasma images but is used for gas injection.

Chamber (Generation 1)

The first generation chamber was different in that it had a far more archaic method for flange-gasket compression. I connected 4 aluminum bars to 4 sets of standoffs, forming crude brackets that I slid on the chamber and cinched down. There were a number of issues with this setup that led me to make the improvements seen in generation 2 above. The first was ease of use. The brackets were extremely tedious to get on and off, meaning it was very hard to switch between the multiple feedthrough flanges I had made. The second, more important issue, was uniform gasket compression and leak detection. The brackets were hard to keep in one place on the faces of the chamber, meaning that it was almost impossible to compress the gasket perfectly. This made leak detection a major issue because I never knew whether the cause of the leak was bracket alignment or an actual connection. This was resolved in the 2nd generation by replacing the old feedthrough flanges with larger square aluminum plates that could accept 4 fixed bolts.

2.1kv DC @200 mA power supply

This 1st generation HV system consists of a variable water resistor for current control, a variac, and a center tapped 2.1kv transformer I sourced from a microwave. This feeds into a full bridge rectifier made from diodes I also took out of a microwave. Finally, two 1uF microwave oven capacitors in parallel smooth the still fluctuating rectified DC to create a more âtrueâ DC waveform. This provides the negative polarity 2.1kvdc needed for the device. Gas injection system

I am currently working on a gas injection system for my fusor. The idea is to inject small amounts of liquid though a needle onto a reactive substance in a small glass container that produces the gas a I want. The gas then is let into the fusor chamber though a valve. I have already done this with a far less advanced setup using sodium hydroxide and aluminum: 2Al + 2NaOH + 2H2O â 2NaAlO2 + 3H2. The resulting "2NaAlO2", or sodium aluminate just stays solid in the reaction chamber and dose not interfere, and only the hydrogen gas I want flows out into the chamber. With my new setup I hope to react Deuterium oxide (heavy water) with Calcium: 2D20 + Ca â Ca(OD)2 + D2. As you can see here I get the pure deuterium gas I want while the oxgen and Ca remain bound as a solid. I am still collecting parts for this system but one thing I have done so far is create a automatic ball valve opener. I did this because I need to control the flow of D2 into the system but I also want to avoid touching anything even remotely connected to my 20kV power supply. The likleyhood that I would get shocked is low, but if I did I would die so it is definitley worth the effort. I borrowed some aluminum scrap from my schools robotics team and created a stand that held the ball valve in place. I then squeezed the handle of the valve inbetween two metal plates to connect it to the motor shaft. I needed a VERY slow high torque motor for this application so I could finley control how "open" the valve was. For this I decided to use a microwave turn table motor. If you think about the glass plate at the bottom of your microwave, it turns incredibly slowly and with a lot of torque. So this motor was perfect. I simply secured it in place and attached the rotor to the handle using the two metal plates. I then used some doorbell wire to lengthen the distance I could operate it from. This requires no motor control because the motor for some reason changes directions when it hits high resistance. So when the valve is all the way open or all the way closed, all I have to do to get it to turn the other way is to give it some power. See a video of it working below. You can see how it automatically switches directions.

Spectroscopy

Another ongoing prodject I am working on that works with my gas injection system is a hommade webcam spectrometer. It works by using a small piece of diffraction grating to split up light into bands wich then are seen by a webcam. A raspberry pi then runs a program (my cousin did the coding) and interpets the bands to produce and inensity/wavelength graph. So what does this have to do with this prodject?

As a plasma, every compound has a it's own unique emission spectrum. So by doing spectroscopy on the plasma the chamber, I can determine the composition of the gas inside. This is important because when I start injecting gasses like D2, I need to know how pure they are. Given I am using heavy water to make my D2 gas, one important impurity that this will help me look out for is oxygen. I have also been talking to other people who have used this gas injection system, and they have also found CO emissions, wich they think is from the mineral oil that the calcium is stored in when it's sent in the mail. So basically all this data will help me improve my prossess and reduce impurities.

Below is the build prossess. I basically made a small wood box and spraypainted it black. I then added some rollers at the top that allow me to have a removable cardboard top. In the back of the box is the camera set at an angle. The difraction grading sits midway down the box. In the front sits a pice of cardboard with a slit that only lets a small band of light through.
