4" Racing Drone

Seen above is the 4" homemade racing drone I made for only $90. The drone is extremely fun to fly and can reach speeds up to 100mph. The build uses 4 Samguk 2700kv motors ($8 each), 4 (???) ESCs ($? each), 4 basic polycarbonate props (75C each), 1 omnibus F4 flight controller ($24), 1 FlySky 2.4ghz receiver ($12), and one basic 4" carbon fiber drone frame ($15).

Soldering Flight controller, ESCs, and Motors

I started trimming the motor wires to the right length and stripping them. I then tinned the ESCs solder pads and attached the motors. Next I trimmed and desolded the unused signal wire on the other side of the ESCs and removed their plastic casing to provide better direct air-cooling during flight. After that I tinned the pads on the flight controler and trimmed the ESCs remaining signal cable. I then soldered the positive, ground, and signal cables from the ESC to the flight controler.

Soldering Battery Connector

The flight controller comes without a battery cable, so I made that as well. I used a few inches of low gauge stranded copper wire from a microwave, soldered it to a female XT60 connector, and finished it off with shrink wrap to prevent any shorts. I tinned the battery pads on the flight controler and then soldered the other side of the cables on.

Receiver

The final soldering I did on the flight controller was for the receiver. I first located the positive, ground, and 3 different signal options on the board using the manufacturing diagram. The 3 options for signal were I-Bus, S-Bus, PPM. All of these options were entirely compatible with my receiver and my flight controller, but I ultimately ended up using PPM because I had a really hard time getting I-Bus and S-Bus to work on the software side. After I decided on my 3 pins I soldered the stripped ends of a 3 wire female connector to the board. The connector then plugged into the respective positive, ground, and PPM leads on the receiver.

Making Charger

The LiPO battery charger I have requires a 12 to 24 volt DC input but doesn't have its own step-down transformer or rectifier, so I had to make my own power supply. I did this using an old laptop charger which already had a 24VDC output. I cut off the plug that goes into the computer and stripped the cable, which strangely had 4 cables. I used my multimeter to determine which were positive and negative and then cut off the extras. I then soldered on a male XT60 connector (so it could plug in to the battery charger) and added shrink wrap.

Antenna Connections

Because I bought the frame with all needed parts, the main mechanical challenge in building this drone was securing the antennas. Securing the antennas is essential because the drone moves violently throughout a flight and if the antennas break loose they will be shredded by the propellers. However if they are secured too tightly in a vulnerable position, they are often the first to break during a crash. I solved both these issues with the setup shown above. Each of these antenna holders consists of sheet aluminum base and a modified plastic spacer. I first drilled out the pacer to make its internal diameter fit that of the antennas. I then shaved the bottoms flat so they could be secured to the base. I cut the base out of thin aluminum from a microwave cover, and drilled two holes on either side so screws could run through them into the standoffs. I then glued the spacers onto the base at a 45 degree angle to match the back legs of the drone. This gave the 2 antennas the 90 degree total angle needed to maximize range. It also kept the antennas snug up against the much stronger legs of the drone, thus making it virtually impossible for them to directly hit the ground in the event of a crash.

Software

I chose to run Betaflight on my flight controller, as it is a very powerful software that is relatively easy to use. I flashed my ESCs using BLHeli because it was the manufacturer's recommended firmware. BLHeli made switching motor directions very convenient, which was great because I had a lot of trouble with the rotors turning the wrong way early on.

Competitive Robotics

I have done competitive robotics with VEX team 8000 for 6 years now. In my time on the team I have built (and sometimes programmed) over 20 large robots all with 5 or more different functions. Over the course of 1 month in the 2017-2018 season, A team I led was ranked #2 in the world for our skills score. Here are some of the robots I worked on with descriptions:

VEX Tower Takeover 11 StackBot

This robot is meant to pick up and vertically stack up to 11 cubes for the VEX Tower Takeover. It uses two rollers driven by two motors to intake and elevate the stack. The tray that holds the cubes is divided into 3 18ï¿½ sections that fold inward to keep the robot within 18ï¿½ by 18ï¿½ by 18ï¿½ at the beginning of the match. The folding works by having each successive section be ~2mm thinner than the last, and external hinges, which allows them to fold neatly into each other. The fold out is powered passively using preloaded elastic force which Is released by the movement of the outer arms. The two outer arms that the rollers mount on are independent from the tray and attached to a single motor at a 1:7 gear ratio, allowing for the arm to lift up multiple feet to place single cubes in elevated containers. Finally the tray is moved to its vertical orientation by a 1 joint arm powered by a single motor geared 1:21. Finally, the robot drives using a 4 motor chassis chained together to eliminate the effect of motor strength inconsistencies.ï¿½ See Images of the robot below.

VEX In The Zone Skills Bot

This robot was primarily designed to be successful in the skills challenge, which it was. This robot was ranked #2 in the world for about a month in the 2017-2018 season. Because it was meant for skills, my design focus was on simplicity, efficiency, and speed. The chassis is a simple 6 motor tank drive with wheels and motors chained together to reduce the effects of motor strength inconsistencies. They were geared to high speed to make the robot faster. The robots objective was to pick up the 4 pound caps and place them in the scoring zones. The pronged arm in the front was built to rest a few mm off the ground and grab the underside of the cones. This based height was also regulated with a potentiometer. The arm lifts the cones off the ground where they then slide into the storage tray. The arm lifts using an asymmetric 4-bar driven by 2 low speed motors geared 7:1. The asymmetry of the 4bar means that the fork and cone do not stay level and gradually lean back as the bar lifts up. This allows for the cone to slide nicly down the angled tray. The tray consists of some metal c-channel at a ~35 degree angle. It is covered in polycarbonate sheeting to lower the friction between itself and the cone. Since the robot was meant to hold up to 2 cones at once, a removable stopping mechanism was made. It is basically just a very rigid axle extension that forms a wall and stops the cone when it is vertical, and gets completely out of the way when it is horizontal. The stopper is driven by one motor geared 1:3 connected to a high strength steel axel. Encoders were also attached to the chassis, allowing for very accurate movements to be made consistently. I also made the 1 minute long autonomous program for this robot. I started by making basic adjustable encoder functions line by line to do things like lift the arm, engage and disengage the stopper, turn left, drive forward ext. From then on creating the program involved figuring out what functions to call in what order and to what degree. After that I had to optimize everything for speed and consistency to make it within the 1 min and 3 try limit. Finally to give the robot some edge in match competition, a flip out arm was added. When deployed the U-Shaped arm prevents the opposing team from getting 2 cones that are vital for victory. By doing this we could prevent even the best teams from winning because they couldn't even use the game objects their robots were designed for. This was so effective that we defeated the defending world champions in an elimination match at worlds. The arm deploys using preloaded elastic force which is triggered by a motor based mechanical release. See Images of the robot above

VEX Startstruck Clawbot

This robot was meant to carry out the VEX Starstruck challenge, which involved throwing large heavy game objects over a 3ft fence onto the opponent's side. It has a simple chained 4 motor high speed chassis. To throw the objects, a lightweight 4 motor arm geared 1:7 is connected to the back chassis tower. The motors are assisted on the upswing by elastic bands which store energy as the arm goes down. The objects are grasped using a large pneumatic claw made of aluminum c-channel. The pneumatics are fueled by two small air tanks pressurized to ~100psi. This gives the claw far more than enough grasp cycles to make it through the typical 2 minute match. To fit the size requirements, the whole claw is placed on a hinge, allowing it to fold backwards. When the arm lifts at the beginning of the match the claw flips out and is held in place by a passive locking mechanism. Finally, a passively deployed Tip-Guard flips out and mechanically locks providing stability, preventing a backwards tip when throwing a heavy load.

VEX Turning Point Double Catapult Double Intake Bot

I made this robot to compete in the Vex turning point game. Its purpose was to intake small plastic balls and precisely shoot them to hit flags stationed on the field. It also needed to flip plastic disks and climb a central platform. Early in the season I realized 2 things: the first was that the game was highly defence based and physical, and second was that being able to hit 2 flags simultaneously was a huge advantage. Because of this I decided to make an extremely strong, tank-like bot that could play good defence and also do well on offence by shooting 2 balls at once. While most teams had intakes on one side of their bot, I had huge intakes on both sides. This provided a huge advantage in terms of being able to rapidly collect balls because the size of the intakes gave a huge margin of error when driving, and also allowed for omni-directional operation (if there was a ball on both sides of the robot, we could simply drive forward and backwards quickly, instead of turning around). The Intakes also provided a much more important role than just picking up balls. I built the intakes using thick axles and chose polycarbonate instead of the typical rubber bands. In addition I used 3 motors for my intake where other teams used only one. Because of this the intake made the robot almost unbeatable defense wise. By running the intake backwards, the polycarbonate sheets would literally lift the opposing team off the ground, making it impossible for them to resist our pushes. In addition, the polycarbonate easily shredded the rubber band intake of any team who dared to come near us. This effect allowed us to win the semi-finals of the California state championship, as a team that would have normally beaten us was rendered useless when we broke their intake, making it impossible for them to pick up balls. Our scoring ability was also very high, as the double catapult was extremely consistent. We also boasted a very high skills score.

VEX Starstruck Passive stacker bot

This Robot was meant to compete in the VEX In The Zone game. Itï¿½s goal is to stack cones on the mobile goals and place them in the scoring zones. It has a 4 motor chassis with a very lightweight build. It grabs and elevates the mobile coals with a tray very similar to the skills bot presented earlier. The cones are picked up using a 1 motor roller that directs them into a passive polycarbonate gripper that mechanically locks on to the contours of the cone. The cone is then added to the stack with a 4 bar that moves the gripper from its intake position to above the mobile goal. In a downward sweeping potion the arm pushes the cone onto the stack to the point that the passive lock disengages, and the arm can move back to the intake position. As the stack grows vertically, a 3 motor double reverse 4 bar elevates the stacker to continue adding to the top. Itï¿½s lifting speed is assisted by elastic bands between the first and second 4 bars that store energy as the lift goes down and release it when they go up. The nature of the 4 bar also ensures that the stacker is always perfectly aligned above the mobile goal regardless of its height. The robot's two batteries are placed on hinged arms on the back which combat the weight of the stack in the front. Finally a elastically powered 1-use cone shooter is mounted on the top to disrupt the opponent at the beginning of the match. One single preload cone is placed on itï¿½s sled and is shot at the other team when the preloaded elastic bands accelerate it.
